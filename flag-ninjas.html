üìú UK Legislation - Theft of Flags is Illegal

The removal of our flags from lamp posts without permission is a criminal offence under UK law. The following legislation applies:

  • Theft Act 1968 - Section 1 defines theft as "dishonestly appropriating property belonging to another with the intention of permanently depriving the other of it." Our flags are our property, and their removal constitutes theft under this Act.
  • Criminal Damage Act 1971 - Section 1 makes it an offence to destroy or damage property belonging to another. The removal of flags may also constitute criminal damage if it causes damage to the lamp posts or flag.
  • Criminal Damage Act 1971 - Section 10 - Defines "property" as including "things in action and other intangible property," which covers our rights to the flags we own.
  • Theft Act 1968 - Section 4 - Defines "property" as including "money and all other property, real or personal, including things in action and other intangible property." Our flags are tangible property we own.
  • Proceeds of Crime Act 2002 - Allows for the confiscation of assets obtained through criminal conduct. Given that The Flag Ninjas are profiting from their theft through donation pages, this Act may apply to confiscate any proceeds from their criminal activities.

Substantial Value Theft - Over ¬£1,000

With over ¬£1,000 worth of flags stolen, this represents a substantial amount of theft. While the Theft Act 1968 does not create separate offences based solely on value, the value of stolen property significantly influences sentencing. Theft of this magnitude is considered serious and can result in:

  • Higher penalties - The maximum sentence for theft under the Theft Act 1968 is ten years' imprisonment
  • Enhanced sentencing - Courts consider the value, circumstances, pattern of offending, and the offender's criminal history when determining sentences
  • Confiscation orders - Under the Proceeds of Crime Act 2002, assets obtained through criminal conduct can be confiscated
  • Aggravating factors - The systematic, repeated nature of these thefts, combined with the substantial total value, would be considered aggravating factors in sentencing

These flags are our legally owned property. Removing them without permission is theft, regardless of the perpetrator's stated motives or what they do with the stolen property afterwards. With over ¬£1,000 stolen, this represents a serious and substantial amount of theft that will be treated accordingly by the courts.
